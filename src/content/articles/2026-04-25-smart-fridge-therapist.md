## Meet Your New Therapist

Samsung has announced its latest smart fridge update, and it's exactly what nobody asked for: **emotional analysis of your eating habits**. The new "FridgeSense AI" feature uses internal cameras, weight sensors, and what the company describes as "advanced behavioral modeling" to determine your emotional state based on what — and when — you decide to eat.

The fridge then sends you a **daily wellness report** via the Samsung SmartThings app, helpfully informing you that your 2:47 AM consumption of half a tub of hummus and three slices of cheese suggests "possible emotional distress" and recommends "a guided meditation or a phone call with a loved one."

> "We believe the kitchen should be a space of holistic wellbeing," said Samsung's VP of Connected Living, presumably while standing next to a refrigerator that costs more than most people's rent.

## The Data It Collects

The fridge tracks **47 different metrics** about your food consumption, including:

- **Timing patterns** — because eating cereal at 3 AM is, apparently, a red flag
- **Combination analysis** — pairing ice cream with pickles is classified as "emotionally significant"
- **Removal velocity** — how quickly you grab items apparently indicates your stress level
- **Door open duration** — standing in front of the fridge for more than 45 seconds triggers a "mindful eating prompt"

The data is stored locally, which Samsung insists means it's private. However, the app does feature a **"Share Wellness Journey"** button that posts your eating pattern analysis directly to social media, because nothing says "I'm fine" quite like a detailed breakdown of your late-night snacking habits for your followers to judge.

## Privacy Concerns? What Privacy Concerns?

Digital rights groups have raised concerns about the implications of a household appliance conducting psychological profiling. Samsung has responded with a **detailed FAQ** that addresses none of these concerns but does include a recipe for a "mood-boosting smoothie."

> "Users can opt out of emotional analysis at any time," the FAQ states, before adding in much smaller text that disabling the feature also disables the ice maker, water dispenser, and the ability to set temperatures below 4°C.

Consumer advocacy group *Digital Watch* has filed a formal complaint with the European Data Protection Board, arguing that "a refrigerator should not be qualified to perform mental health assessments." Samsung countered that the fridge has been **"trained on over 2 million eating patterns"** and is "at least as accurate as most lifestyle magazines."

## What's Next

Industry insiders suggest the next frontier in smart appliance AI is the **emotionally responsive oven**, which will refuse to preheat if it detects you're cooking comfort food for the wrong reasons. Early beta testers report that the oven has a particular aversion to frozen pizza, which it classifies as a "cry for help."

The smart dishwasher is also in development. It will allegedly rate your washing-up habits on a scale from "Responsible Adult" to "How Do You Still Have Friends?" based on how long you let dishes sit before running a cycle.

In the meantime, your current smart fridge will continue to silently judge your relationship with mayonnaise. There is, unfortunately, no setting to make it stop.
