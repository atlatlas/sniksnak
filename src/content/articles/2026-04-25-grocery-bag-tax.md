## Seven Cents of Social Warfare

It started innocently enough. A small surcharge on plastic bags to save the planet. A noble idea, really. But somewhere between the checkout counter and the parking lot, the **grocery bag tax** became something far more powerful than an environmental policy — it became a **character test**.

In the year 2026, the simple act of buying groceries has been elevated to a moral performance. The cashier asks "Do you have a bag?" and suddenly you're not just buying pasta — you're making a statement about who you are as a person.

## The Two Tribes

Society has now divided neatly into two categories:

**The Prepared** — those who carry reusable bags everywhere they go. They have bags in their car, bags in their backpack, a compact bag that folds into a tiny square attached to their keychain. They arrive at the checkout with a satisfied expression, unfolding their organic cotton tote bag with the quiet confidence of someone who has their life together. Their bag says something like "I Love Recycling" or "There Is No Planet B," just to make sure you understand the moral stakes.

**The Unprepared** — those who, despite 8 years of bag taxes, still arrive at the register without a bag every single time. They stand there with a defeated look, muttering "I forgot again" as they pay 7 cents for a plastic bag that will immediately be thrown into a cabinet with 200 other plastic bags. They know this is wrong. They don't care. The 7 cents is, for them, a small price to pay for not having to think about bags.

> "I have a drawer full of reusable bags at home," said one shopper, requesting anonymity. "They're right next to the drawer full of plastic bags. Neither drawer has ever been opened."

## The Psychology of the Bag

Supermarkets have noticed something interesting: **people who bring their own bags also tend to buy more organic produce**, while those who pay for bags tend to gravitate toward frozen pizzas and energy drinks. Researchers are unsure whether the bag causes the behavior or the behavior causes the bag, but the correlation is strong enough that some supermarkets now use bag ownership as a proxy for targeted advertising.

There is also a documented phenomenon of **bag shame** — the momentary feeling of inadequacy when the person behind you in line produces three perfectly organized reusable bags while you fumble with a crumpled plastic one. Studies show this shame lasts approximately **4.7 seconds**, after which it is completely forgotten until the next shopping trip.

## The Collector's Dilemma

Perhaps the most baffling aspect of the bag tax is its effect on household plastic bag collections. Despite paying for bags, nobody throws them away. Every Dutch household contains at least one drawer, cabinet, or closet dedicated entirely to plastic grocery bags. The average household has **approximately 150 bags** at any given time, all stored "just in case," for a situation that has never occurred and will never occur.

Environmental scientists have calculated that if every household in the Netherlands used their stored bags just once before buying new ones, the demand for new plastic bags would drop by **340%**. This finding has been published in several journals and ignored by everyone.

The reusable bag industry, meanwhile, has produced enough cotton tote bags to stretch from Amsterdam to Utrecht and back. Most of these bags are currently sitting in the trunk of a car, waiting to be remembered at the next grocery trip, where they will once again be forgotten.

## The Future

Rumors suggest the government is considering increasing the bag tax to **15 cents**. This has been met with outrage from The Unprepared and indifference from The Prepared, who will not be affected. A third group, previously unidentified in research, has emerged: those who simply **carry their groceries home in their arms** out of principle.

The checkout experience of the future, experts predict, will involve a brief moral questionnaire before payment, asking shoppers to rate their environmental commitment on a scale of 1 to 10. Those scoring below 5 will be publicly shamed via the supermarket's PA system. This is, of course, a joke. Probably.
