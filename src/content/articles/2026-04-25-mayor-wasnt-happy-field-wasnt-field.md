In a stunning display of municipal transparency, the city of **IJsselstein** recently managed to organize a demonstration that was technically peaceful yet emotionally volatile. The event served as proof that a community can express profound dissatisfaction with a football field while ensuring that the local infrastructure remains largely intact.

It is a diplomatic achievement in reverse: inviting people to protest, then telling them where to stand, and finally asking them to go home quietly before they moved elsewhere anyway.

## A Relatively Peaceful Evening

The demonstration began at **Het Podium**, an event site designated by the municipality with what can only be described as bureaucratic optimism. The location served as a holding pen for civic discontent before the crowd decided to migrate to the town hall — a classic move in the protest playbook that suggests the organizers knew exactly where they were going but wanted to make sure everyone was comfortable first.

## The Mayor Gets Honest

**Mayor Esther Weststeijn** took the stage and delivered what can only be described as a moment of rare administrative vulnerability.

> She admitted she had "sometimes been happier."

In an era where politicians are trained to smile through the apocalypse, this admission was met with boos and whistles from the crowd. It is a democratic feedback loop in action, proving that while the Mayor might not be happy about the shelter, her authority is still strong enough to warrant vocal disapproval. One wonders if she would have been happier had she stayed home, but perhaps that would have been too much like democracy.

## Nobody Asked the Football Club

Following this display of transparency, **IJFC chairman Remco Bloemheuvel** took the stage to address what he described as a **"surprising"** decision by the municipality. He noted that a football field is perhaps not the most intuitive location for housing 100 to 150 refugees who have already completed their asylum procedures — a distinction of such bureaucratic precision that it likely saved hours of paperwork.

According to Bloemheuvel, the club was **not consulted** regarding the loss of their pitch, a fact he presented as if it were a minor oversight in an otherwise flawless system. One wonders if the municipality considered asking for permission before turning a sports complex into a temporary village, but perhaps that would have been too much like democracy.

## Asylum Seeker or Refugee?

The irony extends to the terminology used by the protesters. The chairman corrected the crowd: *"They are not asylum seekers, but refugees."* It is a distinction of such immense importance that it likely outweighs the loss of the actual grass. People were booing people who had already finished their paperwork, which suggests that the bureaucracy has become so complex that even the complaints have been processed before they reach the surface.

## The Crowd Had Other Plans

After approximately thirty minutes, the protest at Het Podium concluded with instructions for attendees to *"go home quietly."* The crowd, displaying a notable disregard for these instructions, proceeded to the town hall instead. There, they engaged in a celebratory display of fireworks and chanting slogans against the shelter.

A significant police presence was noted on site, though their primary function appeared to be **observing rather than intervening** — suggesting that the local law enforcement officers were enjoying a rare evening off from actual action.

## Respectful Protesters and the Far Right

The demonstration was organized by the group **"No asylum seekers' center on Groenvliet,"** led by an organizer named **Mike** who called for respectful behavior and collaborative solutions. It is always refreshing to see a protest that includes a preamble about how everyone should treat each other with respect, even while standing in front of a building they are protesting against.

**Defend Netherlands** was also present, bringing their far-right action group credentials to the evening. While previous protests in **Loosdrecht** had involved eggs and projectiles, the IJsselstein branch remained relatively subdued. By 21:00, the square in front of the town hall had emptied, leaving only a few dedicated members from local branches of Defend Netherlands behind to ensure the night ended with dignity.

## A Historic Achievement in Civic Restraint

In conclusion, the evening proved that while communication may be lacking and football fields may be repurposed for housing, the people of IJsselstein can still manage to agree on being slightly annoyed without causing a major incident. It was a historic achievement in civic restraint, or at least it would have been if anyone had actually listened to the instructions.

The Mayor admitted she wasn't happy, the crowd booed her, and the football club lost its pitch for six months. Everyone got exactly what they wanted: a temporary solution that felt permanent.
